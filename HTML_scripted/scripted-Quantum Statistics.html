Satyendra Nath Bose, Quantum Statistics


Satyendranath Bose had his fist paper on quantum statistics titled "PLANCK'S LAW AND THE LIGHT QUANTUM HYPOTHESIS" published in 'Zeitschrift fur Physik' on 2 July 1924. An English translation was later published in the 'Journal of Astrophysics and Astronomy', Vol. 15, NO. 1/MAR, P. 3, 1994. The referenced translation linked to here is made available by Harvard. It is important to note that other translations exist and some examined contained significant errors. The referenced translation here seems to be accurate but just as with any translation it is always possible that it contains an omission or error. A yellow highlighted bold font is used in this article to identify formulas as they appear in Bose's paper. The highlighting is intended to aid the reader in relating this information to Bose's paper.

In his paper Bose referred to the earlier work by Max Planck in 1901 who derived a relationship between radiation density of a blackbody at thermal equilibrium and the mean energy of an oscillator. Bose credited Planck's work as the starting point for quantum theory. However he indicated that the 8πν2dν/c3 factor in Planck's radiation density formula was derived from classical theory and therefore was unsatisfactory. The main point of Bose's new derivation was to arrive at this same result without the reliance on classical theory, but rather by using a statistical derivation of the density of a black-body spectral emission. His new approach is explained in his 1924 paper. To make his paper a bit more accessible to most readers the following discussion of his 1924 paper is presented. Note that equations that have a yellow highlighted backgrounds are exactly the same equations that Bose presented in his publication. In addition, these equations are presented in the same order that they appear in his publication.

To begin, the reader may benefit from a reference for certain notations used by Bose. To minimize confusion the notations below are those used by Bose in his paper.

dνs incremental change of frequency νs, where the subscript 's' is an index for an individual frequency
ρν the spectral density contribution from quanta having the frequency ν
Αs the number of arrangements of a single quanta within an incremental frequency change dνs
V the volume in which the spectral density is to be computed
E the total energy of the quanta within the volume V
Ns a count of the total number of quanta sharing the same frequency corresponding to the increment of frequency dνs
psx a phase space's unique cell division where the s superscript denotes the quanta frequency and the x subscript denotes the number of quanta within the cell
W The statistical probability of cell populations over all the frequencies dνs

Bose began his paper by stating that the condition that must be met is the existing formula for radiation density as a function of energy. That formula for light density at a specific frequency ν was ρνdν=(8πν2dν / c3)E. This formula states that the spectral density at a specific frequency ρν corresponding to each small increment of frequency dν is equal to the total energy E times the scaling facor (8πν2dν / c3).

Aside:  Don't be confused by the use of 'ν' in the Bose equations for frequency. Planck's equation is sometimes presented using the letter 'f' for frequency, but Planck originally wrote this formula as E=hν. The 'ν' character for frequency tends to be used interchangeably with 'f'. There is a discussion regarding the use of 'ν' or 'f' at The Stack Exchange.

Max Planck had previously established in 1900 that for each quanta with the frequency f the energy would be given by E=hf. Bose based the total energy E having Ns quanta on Plank's formula. He expressed that relationship as E=Σs(Nshνs). That states that the total energy E is the sum over all quanta frequencies. For each frequency the energy contribution is the number of quanta (Ns) at that frequency νs times Plank's energy formula. The summation for total energy E is defined over a range of s, s=0 to s=∞. He equated this summation to the integration formula E=V ∫ρvdv.  The integral means that the total energy E is comprised of all individual spectral densities ρν at each frequency ν times the volume V that contains all of the quanta particles. His explanation established the energy equalities E=Σs(Nshνs)=V ∫ρvdv.  Be aware that in his translated paper they used subscripts and superscripts interchangeably, so Ns is the same as Ns.

Aside:  In the previous paragraph the symbols Σs and ∫ were introduced. The symbol Σs is the mathematical notation for a successive summation over an incremental range of values indexed by the values of s. The s index is generally supplied with both initial and terminal values to define the range of the summation. The symbol ∫ is the mathematical notation for the integration operator. In the case above the integral function is given by 'ρv'. The operator ∫ is often used with a start and end range over which the area is to be computed. The operator performs the area computation within the function's plot with respect to the function's zero axis. That base for the integration is indicated by the appended notation "dx" which is read "the change in x". Integral operators can be presented as multiples like ∫∫∫dx dy dz which would be the arbitrary area of a three dimensional region for a function with changes in x, y and z.

Bose wrote that in order to evaluate the energy equality E=Σs(Nshvs)=V ∫ρvdv, it is necessary to find those numbers Ns for each frequency νs that determine its spectral density ρν. He reasoned that there would be many possible probability distribution curves resulting from the various combinations of Ns values. He pointed out that the solution for the correct value of Ns can be found where the probability distribution curve has a maximum and still satisfies the energy equality. These conditions are the constraints used in finding the final form of the derivation formula. He added that each quanta has a momentum of ps=hνs/c in the direction of its motion.

Aside:  For some it might be interesting to see how Bose obtained his momentum formula for each quanta. The quanta momentum formula just given is derived from the momentum formula (mass times velocity) p=mV, Planck's law E=hν and Einstein's E=mc2. Note that in the momentum formula V is Velocity and not the total Volume of Bose's formula. First equate the energy formulas hν=mc2. Then rearrange to obtain an expression for mass as m=hν/c2. Substitute this mass into the momentum formula p=(hν/c2)V. For quanta the velocity V is the speed of light c. The substitution of c for V in p=(hν/c2)V allows the c value introduced in the numerator to cancel one c value of the c2 in the denominator. The final expression is then ps=hνs/c.

Given that each quanta has both a position within V and a momentum that dictates its next position, he characterized each quanta contributing to the spectral density using three location coordinates and three momentum values (one momentum value for each coordinate axis). The three location coordinates define the quanta's position using x, y, and z coordinates. Then using these same coordinate axes he defined the three momentum components as px, py and pz. For any incremental change of the quanta there is a corresponding change in all these six values. Bose stated that these six values comprise the volume of a six dimensional phase space domain. The volume of the phase space domain is equal to the integral of all the incremental changes, ∫ dx dy dz dpx dpy dpz. This six dimensional formula can be viewed as the product of two separate three dimensional formulas.

The triple integral of the three position changes, ∫∫∫dx dy dz, represents a volume V. The triple integral of the three momentum changes, ∫∫∫dpx dpy dpz, is also regarded as a volume within the momentum phase space that is equivalent to the incremental increase of a spherical shape. The three momentum components all change from their initial vector magnitudes p to new magnitudes corresponding to the change p+dp. The spherical increase in the vector space describes a shell in the three positional dimensions. The spherical shell shape of the incremental increase permits the change without regard to a specific direction. The volume of the spherical shell is obtained by multiplying the surface area of the sphere[1] having a radius of p times the thickness of the shell which is the incremental increase dp. This then gives the formula ∫∫∫dpx dpy dpz=4π(p2) dp. As previously stated, Bose defined the magnitude of the momentum ps as hνs/c. Substituting the value of ps into the integral equation yields ∫∫∫dpx dpy dpz=4π(hν/c)2 d(hν/c). Bose stated that the quanta particle's frequency forces it to remain on the cylindrical surface. He stated that there is then a correspondence between a change in the frequency domain (dν) to a change in the phase space domain (dpxyz). He wrote the formula for the quanta's momentum change as ∫∫∫dpx dpy dpz=4π(hν/c)2 ((h dν)/c). By squaring the individual members of the (hν/c)2 term and combining the like terms, the final form of the equation can be written as 4π(h3ν2/c3) dν. He concluded this part of his analysis by combining the volume times the momentum to depict the volume of the phase space domain as a function of frequency as ∫ dx dy dz dpx dpy dpz=4π(h3ν2/c3) V dν. This final formula equates the frequency domain equivalent to his phase space domain.

Bose then divided up the phase space into individual cells. His proposed set of cells represented the number of possible arrangements of individual quantum particles having the frequency ν positioned within the phase space volume. This approach was significant to his derivation. By treating quantum particles as being indistinguishable he departed from the prevailing wisdom of his time which arithmetically treated quantum particles as unique hertzian oscillators. To develop the statistical treatment Bose created a method to implement counts and probable distributions.

He stated that the number of phase space cells must match the total number of possible arrangements for each quantum particle within the phase space. The minimal energy change of a photon was given by Max Planck's equation 'E=hν'. Planck had previously established the constant h as the discrete incremental change of energy for each quantum. Bose used the minimal energy change to assign the volume of each cell of the phase space as h3. To compute the total number of unique cells in the domain per frequency νs he divided the frequency domain formula equivalent of the phase space by the h3 volume to obtain 4π(ν2/c3)V dν. Curiously, Bose stated in this article, "Concerning the kind of subdivision of this type, nothing definitive can be said." He then indicated that to account for polarization, the count formula must be multiplied by 2. On page 3 of his article Bose wrote the final formula for the number of unique cells in phase space for a given frequency νs as 8π(ν2s/c3)V dνs. On page 4 Bose established the equation for the variable Αs=8π(ν2s/c3) dνs. It is subtle point to make, but observe that in this definition of Αs it is not distributed over the volume V.

Having defined his mathematical model Bose then began his statistical derivation. Given Ns quanta within each increment of frequency dνs, the question that he next addressed was in how many ways can the Ns quanta be distributed into the Qs number of cells? To start, he defined a sequence of cell types such that for each frequency νs the cells can be defined as ps0 (a cell that contains no quanta), ps1 (a cell that contains one quanta), ps2 (a cell that contains two quanta), and so on. The sum of this series is equal to the total number of quanta sharing the same frequency corresponding to the increment of frequency dνs. This is expressed by the formula Ns=0ps0 + 1ps1 + 2ps2 + 3ps3 + …. The sum of terms in this expression can be rewritten using the summation operator Σ as Ns=Σr(rpsr). Bose refers to this summation form later in his derivation process.

Bose explained that for each frequency change dν, the number of ways that the total quanta can be distributed into these cell types without regard to order is given statistically by the binominal coefficient[2] 'nCk'. Using the binominal coefficient to determine the number of ways quanta with the same frequency νs can populate the total kinds of cells was expressed by the equation Αs! / ps0! ps1! ps2!….

Aside:  The German mathematician Michael Stifel[3] is credited with providing the first documentation of the binominal coefficient in 1544 in his "Arithmetica Integra".

In statistics the specific multiplication rule[4] is used to compute the probability of multiple independent events. Applying the specific multiplication rule, the formula to compute the probability of any given state resulting from all the probabilities over all the frequencies νs is given by W=Πs(Αs! / ps0! ps1! ps2!…). The 'W' variable is introduced on page 4 in a manner that might be confusing to the reader and so this variable's treatment was clarified in this review of his paper. Bose pointed out that the terms psr can be viewed as large numbers. By this distinction he states that it is acceptable to use the Stirling Approximation[5] for the factorial operators with large numerical values. Applying the Stirling Approximation he presents the new form ln(W)=Σs(Αsln(Αs)) - Σs(Σr(psr ln(psr))).

Aside:  The probability formula contains the mathematical operators ! and Πs. Some may not be familiar with these operators. The ! operator is called the factorial. It is a repeated operation restricted to positive integer values that successively multiplies the integer values starting at 1 through n. So n! is 1x2x3x...xn. The Π operator is called the product operator. It is a repeating operation that performs the product of a sequence of values. In Bose's formula the probability is the product of all possible distributions for every frequency νs.

Some explanation is needed to explain how Bose used the Stirling Approxiation to produce the new form. The initial formula W=Πs(Αs! / ps0! ps1! ps2!…) can also be expressed as W=Πs(Αs! / Πr(psr!)) by gathering the series multiplication in the denominator. The successive products of fractions is equivalent to the product of the numerators divided by the product of the denominators. Using this simple rule the equation becomes W=Πs(Αs!) / Πs(Πr(psr!)). Using the rules of logarithms[6] the formula for W can be simplified.

Aside:  Exponentiation and logarithms may be unfamiliar to some. The logarithm and exponent functions are related mathematical operators. An exponent is an argument to a numeric value in some base (typically base 10) that specifies a count of multiplications of the value by the base such as 5(10)E+1=50. The logarithm has the opposite effect of an exponent in that it produces a value less than its argument. By definition the 'logbase(N)=Result' is equivalent to baseResult=N. So the logarithm is a function on a numeric base that specifies the count of the number of divisions by the base that exist in the argument. For example log(10)(100)=2.

Bose took the natural logarithm[7] of both sides of the equation for W to produce the new equation ln(W)=ln(Πs(Αs!)/Πs(Πr(psr!)). Next, applying the logarithmic identity 'logb(x/y)=logb(x)-logb(y)' it is possible to rewrite the equations as ln(W)=ln(Πs(Αs!)) - ln(Πs(Πr(psr!))). The product series of the Π operator then becomes a summation series Σ by applying the logarithmic identity 'logb(xy)=logb(x) + logb(y)'. The equation can be written ln(W)=Σs(ln(Αs!)) - Σs(ln(Πr(psr!))). The same logarithmic rule can be applied to ln(Πr(psr!)). Bose's final distribution probability equation form was then ln(W)=Σs(ln(Αs!)) - Σs(Σr(ln(psr!))).

By applying the Stirling Approximation to the previous formula's factorial operators it can be rewritten as ln(W)=Σs(Αs ln(Αs) - Αs) - Σs(Σr(psr ln(psr) - psr )). The variable Αs was defined by Bose to represent the total number of arrangements of a single quanta with a given frequency change dνs. The total number of arrangements that the variable Αs represents must be equal to the total count of quanta distributed into the cells. To show this equality he wrote that Αs=Σr(psr). Using the rule Σ(a-b)=Σ(a)-Σ(b) allows Σs(Αs ln(Αs) - Αs) - Σs(Σr(psr ln(psr) - psr )) to be rewritten as Σs(Αs ln(Αs)) - Σs(Αs) - Σs(Σr(psr ln(psr)) - (-Σs(Σr(psr ))). The forth term -(-Σs(Σr(psr ))) becomes +Σs(Σr(psr )). Because Αs=Σr(psr) the second term in the equation -Σs(Αs) becomes -Σs(Σr(psr )). As a result the second and forth terms cancel each other. The final form of this equation is then written as ln(W)=Σs(Αs ln(Αs)) - Σs(Σr(psr ln(psr))).

By this point in his paper Bose has established a counting model based on the theoretical number of cells within a phase space. The cells represent a range of change in individual discrete frequencies over the spectrum of quanta present within the phase space volume. He established that more than one quanta can occupy a single cell space, which means that these quanta are not distinguishable from one another. To establish his final solution he pointed out that the probability of cell populations over all the frequencies defined by the formula ln(W) must be solved for the maximum of the probability distribution. To express this maximum probability he used the optimization method called the Lagrange multiplier[8].

Aside:  The Lagrange method (Lagrange Multipliers) is a method for finding a local minimum or maximum for a function. The Lagrange method is used when functionally related constraints are defined. The method is credited to Lagrange because of his book published in 1788 on analytical approaches to mechanics titled 'Mécanique Analytique'. In his book he presented an analytic alternative for solving mechanics problems in place of the geometric approach explained in Netwon's 1687 publication 'Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica'. Lagrange's analytical method for statics was to determine the STATIC STATE that exists when the applied forces are comprised of arbitrary forces. In the static case the net sum of the applied forces must be zero. In a similar manner the statistical maximum of a distribution sought by Bose is also a static system in thermal equilibrium consisting of related functions whose sum of the derivatives must be equal to zero. So the method of undetermined coefficients employed by Lagrand to solve the static state is applied by Bose to find the maximum.
     There is an English translation of Lagrange's book titled 'Analytical Mechanics' by AUGUSTE BOISSONNADE and VICTOR N. V AGLIENTE. In the preface to the translation the authors wrote, "Lagrange's effort was to bring a large portion of what was known about mechanics in his day under one principle - the Principle of Virtual Work." Virtual work was Lagrange's concept of work not actually done, but rather work that would be done if a static system became perturbed. A static system is a stationary state of an object having all applied forces in equilibrium. On page 29 of his book the static state is expressed as the sum of forces along their lines of application to their force center as "P dp+Q dq+R dr+…=0." In SECTION IV page 59 Lagrange explains that "to preserve the equilibrium of the static state various equations of condition [functionally related constraints] are required which are given by the nature of the system." This discussion leads up to his introduction of 'undetermined coefficients' which Lagrange applies to create the 'General Equation of Equilibrium'.
     There is a good discussion regarding the history of the Lagrange multipliers at the stack exchange. For more details there is a paper that was published in the "JOURNAL OF OPTIMIZATION THEORY AND APPLICATIONS" by P. Bussotti titled 'On the Genesis of the Lagrange Multipliers'.

As a first step to apply the Lagrange method Bose recalled the constraints that he established at the beginning of his paper. His first constraint was E=Σs(Nshνs) for the total energy. The second constraint was with regard to the count of the total number of quanta sharing the same frequency corresponding to the increment of frequency dνs. For this he specified that Ns=Σr(rpsr). He next wrote, "Carrying out the variation gives the condition..." It is presumed that by his use of the word 'variation' Bose meant that the variables of the function formula he obtained for ln(W) must vary in relation to the constraints just mentioned to locate the maximum of the system. That variation then depends on several parameters of the ln(W) function.

It is necessary to find the values of these parameters for which the expectation value of the energy is the highest possible. Each of the parameters would produce a maximum where the derivative of its function is zero. The derivative of any function is the expression of the slope of the tangent at every point on the function. Where the tangential slope of the function becomes zero the function is horizontal to the its base axis. This happens at a maximum or a minimum as the local function value trend reverses, either decreasing or increasing.

To find the desired maximum for ln(W) he used three inter-dependent functions. He set each of their individual derivatives to zero in order to define their separate maximums: Σs(Σr(δpsr(1+ln(psr ))))=0, Σs(δNshνs)=0 and Σr(δpsr )=0. In the first and third derivative functions the change is with respect to a change of the values of the cell divisions psr . In the second derivative function the change is with respect to the total number of quanta sharing the same frequency Ns, however he reminds the reader that δNs=Σr(rδpsr ). So substituting Σr(rδpsr ) for δNs in the second equation then completes a set of three equations maximized with respect to a change in psr .

Precisely how Bose obtained his first derivative function could not be determined for this article. It appears that the first derivative function may have been obtained by some variation of the ln(W) formula. He may have found a way to eliminate some terms because the derivative of a constant is always zero. The second derivative function maximizes his first constraint on the total energy E. The third derivative function maximizes the number of arrangements of a single quanta at each increment of frequency As. So Bose completed the preliminary step to the Lagrange method by establishing three interdependent derivative equations where each changes incrementally with respect to the same variable.

Bose substituted Σr(rδpsr ) for δNs in Σs(δNshνs) to produce the equation Σs(Σr(rδpsr )hνs). He rearranged the product within the summation Σs() as Σs(hνs Σr(rδpsr )). Using this new derivative formula and the first and third derivative formulas he multiplied these three derivative formulas with Lagrange multipliers and summed them. Applying the Lagrange multipliers (1), λ and 1/β produced the equation (1)Σs(Σr(δpsr(1+ln(psr )))) + Σs(λs Σr(δpsr)) + 1/β(Σs(hνs Σr(rδpsr ))). By combining the first and second terms the final equation becomes Σs(Σr(δpsr(1+ln(psr ) + λs))) + 1/β(Σs(hνs Σr(rδpsr )))=0.

At this point of his paper Bose stated "From this we get the next step..." and introduces the equation psr =Bsexp(-rhνs/β). Note that at this point the translators introduced a new variable 'Bs' without definition. It cannot be explained here how this derivation was performed. Given that Einstein had reviewed and approved Bose's derivation lends sufficient trust that the work is valid. However, in the periodical 'Current Science, Vol. 69 (10 July 1995)' published by Current Science Association is an article by S.K Das and S. Sengupta titled "Professor S. N. Bose's contribution to quantum statistics - A critical review". On page 76 of this article the authors noted about Bose's derivation, "It is really surprising that neither Bose nor Einstein in their first paper considered it necessary to examine critically the derivation of the new expression for thermodynamic probability." There is a hint towards this mysterious step offered in that same article, "In the logarithmic derivative of W, one has to use this condition [that Zs (expressed as As in Bose's article) is constant]". Recall that Αs=Σr(psr). The derivative of a constant is zero.

Substituting the new form of psr  into the Αs equation yields Αs=Σr(Bsexp(-rhνs/β)).  Αs is the count of arrangements of a single quanta having an incremental change in the specific frequency νs. The equation is a sum over all integer values for r from zero to infinity. The general geometric series formula to be applied is Σr(akr)=a[(1-kr)/(1-k)] for r=0 to n. Using the exponent law xab=(xa)b the Αs formula's 'k' value can be expressed as exp(-hνs/β)-r. Since the exponent value r is negative the value exp(kr) will vanish as r becomes large. In the general formula it can be seen that when kn becomes zero the formula in this case becomes essentially a[(1)/(1-k)]. The sum for Αs becomes Bs[1/(1-exp(-hνs/β))]. Solving for the constant Bs (written Bs) gives Bs=Αs[1-exp(-hνs/β)].

At this point Bose recalls that Ns=Σr(rpsr). By using the previous equation psr =Bsexp(-rhνs/β) the equation Ns=Σr(rpsr) becomes Ns=Σr(rBsexp(-rhνs/β)). Substituting for Bs then gives Ns=Σr(rΑs[1-exp(-hνs/β)]exp(-rhνs/β)). Using the summation identity Σ(Cf(n))=CΣ(f(n)) the constant value Αs[1-exp(-hνs/β)] can be removed from the summation to give Ns=Αs[1-exp(-hνs/β)]Σr(rexp(-rhνs/β)). Again applying the rules of geometric series for the summation Σr and the convergence as r approaches infinity this becomes Ns=Αs[exp(-hνs/β)] / (1-exp(-hνs/β).

At the end of page 5 Bose concluded the derivation. He first returned to the energy equality E=Σs(Nshνs) established at the beginning of his paper. There is now an expression for the total number of quanta sharing the same frequency corresponding to the increment of frequency Ns (also written Ns). He recalls here the definition Αs=8π(ν2s/c3)dνs. Substituting for the Αs in the Ns equation that was just obtained produces the equation Ns=8π(ν2s/c3)dνs[exp(-hνs/β)/(1-exp(-hνs/β)]. Substituting for Ns in the energy equation then produces E=Σs([8π(ν2s/c3)dνs[exp(-hνs/β)]/(1-exp(-hνs/β)]hνs). Rearranging terms then produces E=Σs([8πhνsν2s]/c3 ([exp(-hνs/β)]/[1-exp(-hνs/β)]dνs). The νs terms combine to ν3s.

Notice the absence of the volume variable V in this equation. Recall that on page 3 Bose stated that the total number of cells must be regarded as the number of possible arrangements of one quantum in the given cell volume. Also recall that on page 4 that Αs was defined without a volume. In his phase space formula the phase space change was comprised of both the increment of momentum and the increment of volume. So the volume must be accounted for. It is not clear whether it was silently added here, originally omitted by the translators or whether Bose considered it part of Αs. The formula for the total energy E given by Bose is finally expressed as E=Σs( [8πhν3s]/c3 V ([exp(-hνs/β)] / [1-exp(-hνs/β)] dνs).

Bose next examined the entropy[9] in the system. He wrote the entropy as S=k{(E/β)-Σs(Αs)ln[1-exp(hνs/β)]}. Recall that Bose's article is examining the energy distribution in black body radiation from a system that is at thermal equilibrium. In his system the energy is given by the sum of all individual energies from microstates at each frequency change given by E=Σs(f(νs)dνs). The entropy is based on Boltzmann's equation S=kLog(W) where S is entropy, k is Boltzmann's constant, and W is the total number of possible microstates. Bose concludes that because the change in entropy is equal to the change in energy divided by the constant temperature ∂S/∂E=1/T then it is correct to conclude that the β variable in his energy equation is equal to kT. This then allows that the Bose statistical approach produces the same result as the Planck's formula E=Σs((8πhν3s/c3) V [exp(hνs/kT)-1]-1dνs).

Aside:  Some readers may be curious how entropy fits this discussion. The importance arises in two ways. In the classical physics the understanding of heat transfer systems were written as the laws of thermodynamics. The first law is the law of the conservation of energy which states that energy of the isolated system can not be created or destroyed. Given this law it is then necessary to account for the difference between the starting and ending energy. This is stated in the second law of thermodynamics as entropy. So for the system state change modeled by Bose the energy change results in an entropy value. He represents the entropy in terms of his micro states Ns.
     The second importance of entropy to Bose's derivation arises in its prior derivation as a statistical distribution by Ludwig Boltzmann. Around 1872 Ludwig Boltzmann extended Maxwell's statistical analysis of molecular velocities to entropy. In 1877 he published the paper 'On the Relationship between the Second Fundamental Theorem of the Mechanical Theory of Heat and Probability Calculations Regarding the Conditions for Thermal Equilibrium'. An English translation of Boltzmann's paper can be obtained from here. Regarding this paper, Dieter Flamm wrote on page 6 of his article 'History and outlook of statistical physics' that in Boltzmann's prior work, "He could show that the entropy S is proportional to the 6N–dimensional phase space volume Ω occupied by the corresponding macrostate of an N–particle system". The similarity between this conceptual model and Bose's model are clear.

  Neither the dot product nor the inner product operator is associative because the dot and inner product operations between a vector and a scalar is not defined. Each operator is only valid between two vectors. However there is a kind of associative definition involving a scalar as shown. The equality is a combination of the multiply and dot product or inner product operators. A vector's magnitude is scaled by multiplication with a scaler. For this associative equality to hold either A or B can be scaled but not both. The right side of the equality shown scales the B vector by a, but it also holds if the A vector is scaled.
  The inner product has its origin in Hamilton's quest for multiplication of complex valued vectors. The imaginary component of the complex valued vector presents a special case for this operation. In the case of the dot product from which the inner product is derived, two real valued vectors are the terms of the operation and the result is a single number. That number represents what magnitude of the first real valued vector exists in the same direction as the second vector. Ideally the Inner Product binary operator should be commutative the same as the Dot Product, however the commutative property only holds for the complex conjugate of the commuted operation.
