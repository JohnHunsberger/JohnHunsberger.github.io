Electron's Quantum Properties, A History


There are two historical threads presented in this article of the individuals who played important roles towards understanding the physics and mathematics of the electron quantum particle. The physics and mathematics are intertwined so that progress in one cannot be appreciated without considering the progress in the other. The main thread follows the progress of the physical experiments and the secondary thread follows the mathematical developments needed to model the experimental results. The two threads are differentiated by slightly indenting the mathematical contributors. It is not possible to include all of the historical contributions in this presentation.

[1808] John Dalton  Atoms are defined as measurable particles.
The concept that elements are composed of smaller particles was published around 1808[1] by John Dalton. His work with the 'weights' of gases suggested that there exists a unique atom for each element and that elements exist as aggregates of these fundamental parts. He proposed that atoms are measurable particles and that all elements conform to what he called the system of "atomic weights". The formalization of the idea that all matter is composed of atoms was the first important step towards identifying the subatomic structures and developing theories to explain their properties and behaviors. It is curious how the evidences for the existence of subatomic particles and their behaviors arose from so many different physical investigations, in particular astronomy.

[1809] Joseph-Louis Lagrange and Siméon Denis Poisson  Analysis of elliptical orbits.
Lagrange and Poisson were working on the dynamics of the motion of planets. Kepler proposed in early 1600 that observed planetary motion can be regarded mathematically as a path upon a smooth elliptical surface resulting from a definable mechanical system. Around 1809 both Lagrange[60] and Poisson simultaneously investigated the mathematics for the motion on a curved surface. They used classical mechanics to derive orbitals as a combination of the many forces describing a planet's path. Motion on an eliptical path can be viewed as the result of many small incremental changes from one coordinate to the next. Depicting the motion between two points on the orbital path as having a flat coordinate system allows the incremental change to be described as the consequence of the sum of the incremental changes of the component forces. This kind of local representation is called a manifold[27]. This perspective is similar to viewing a trip from one place to another on a flat road map when in fact the earth is not flat. Their effort resulted in defining the composition laws called the Poisson bracket. The Poisson bracket can be thought of as a binary operation on two sets of coordinates that maps the first set to the second. It has the notation (a, b)[26]. The work by Lagrange and Poisson is extended later by William Hamilton to introduce a new means to express mechanics.
Aside:  The word manifold as used here may be unfamiliar. Manifold in mathematics takes its meaning from the origin of the word "many folds". It serves to convey that the greater surface of which it represents only a localized region, can be expressed as a collection "folds" of such regions[61].
Aside:  The term "composition law" refers to "binary operations". In a general sense it defines the combination of two elements of a single set to produce another element of the same set[22].
Aside:  Poisson introduced a nomenclature depecting paired functions using parethesis (a, b) that is referred to in some literature as the Poisson parenthesis. Because his published notation conflicted with the previous use of parenthesis by Lagrange, his notation was changed by mathematicians to {a, b} and is most commonly referred to as Poisson bracket to differentiate it from the Lagrange parenthesis [23].

[1833] William Hamilton  Hamiltonian Mechanics.
In 1833 William Hamilton created a new mathematical formulation for Newtonian mechanics called Hamiltonian mechanics. The prior work by Poisson played an important role in the Hamiltonian mechanics[24]. Hamilton's new mechanics depicted any change described by the classical Newtonian mechanics as a transition within a coordinate system, from one coordinate position to another. At each coordinate position the total energy is described by two independent energy functions T and V where T+V=ℋ. The 'ℋ' is the Hamiltonian symbol. The Hamiltonian represents the total energy in the system (kinetic 'T' plus potential 'V'). The energy functions T and V are independent of each other such that at time t, the value of T is a function of p, f1(p, t), and the value of V is a function of q, f2(q, t). The result is a three axis depiction of mechanics T, V, and t that represents mechanical change of T and V over time t as a curved path in 3D space. The 3D space is called the phase space because it represents all of the phases of the mechanical system's transitions with respect to time[25]. The transition from one point coordinate system to the next point's coordinate system is expressed by a transformation function.

Aside:  There may be some who may not be familiar with the idea of a 'phase space'. 'phase space' is an abstraction of a theoretical volume in some space that can be defined by the permissible limits on some number of associated values. Consider a set of values for clothing; hat size, shoe size, suit size, tie length, ring size, etc. where each clothing item has an upper and lower bound on the size range. For example a ring size can vary from 3 to 9. The items mentioned are associated by all being worn by a person, though for any one person their set of size values will vary from others. If the set of values considered consisted only of rings then the space for that value range would be a line starting with 3 and ending with 9. If shoes are included with rings, having values from 6 to 16, then the phase space becomes a plane with a volume of 60. If there is a person who has both tight shoes and a loose ring and changes their shoe size from 9 to 10 and their ring from 5 to 4 then two things. The combination of the sizes are within the phase space volume. The person's phase change in the phase space was from (9,5) to (10,4).
Aside:  "In Hamiltonian mechanics, a canonical transformation is a change of canonical coordinates[28] (q, p, t) → (Q, P, t) that preserves the form of Hamilton's equations (that is, the new Hamilton's equations resulting from the transformed Hamiltonian may be simply obtained by substituting the new coordinates for the old coordinates)..."[30].

[1843] William Hamilton  The definition of Quaternians.
The introduction of complex and imaginary numeric values dates back to around 1828 when Carl Friedrich Gauss introduced them[36]. In 1843 Hamilton perceived a difficulty with the arithmetic treatment of the imaginary numbers. He detailed an algebra of couples by defining the usual algebraic operations on these numeric pairs. He then concluded his theory of couples by applying it to imaginary numbers where (a + b√ -1 ) becomes the couple (a,b). By his couples notation √ -1 =(0,1). Hamilton wanted to extend the algebra of couples to three dimensional constructs called triplets. He encountered a difficulty defining multiplication of the imaginary values. In order to complete the required algebraic rules for triplets he was led to the numerical construct he called quaternions. His quaternion structure extended the triplet by one additional dimension. In his article in Philosophical Magazine, (1844–1850) titled "ON QUATERNIONS, OR ON A NEW SYSTEM OF IMAGINARIES IN ALGEBRA" he presented the derivation of a method of multiplication that suspends the pre-existing rule of algebra that multiplication must satisfy the commutative property[37]. The necessary step to accommodate multiplication of imaginary couples and preserve a symmetry in the product (Commutative law) was to define that the product of two complex valued pairs requires forming the complex conjugate of the second term. This advanced the idea that the multiplication of two imaginary numbers ij is not equal to ji but rather -ji. Hamilton's quaternians were popular for awhile but were supplanted later by Josiah Gibbs in 1884 who presented the same operations using vectors[63].

[1858] Arthur Cayley and James Sylvester  The definition of matrix algebra.
The combined work of Arthur Cayley and James Sylvester's established an algebra based on matrices. The matrix algebra produced was an extension to the existing algebraic means for the solution of simultaneous linear equations. In the mid 1800's the collaboration between Arthur Cayley and James Joseph Sylvester on simultaneous linear equations produced a formal mathematical treatment for what Sylvester named the "matrix". In 1858 Cayley published an article in the Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society of London (pp. 17-37) titled 'Memoir on the Theory of Matrices'. This publication introduced his theory of matrices for which he stated, "It will be seen that matrices ... comport themselves as single quantite; they may be added, multiplied or compounded together...". In the introduction Cayley illustrates that the matrix content is composed from the coefficients of a set of linear equations. On pg. 31 paragraph number 36, Cayley discusses how one matrix can transpose another. In this article he gave the transpose matrix the symbol "tr.". Modern mathematics typically uses the the term 'T' to represent the transpose matrix.
Aside:  There may be some who are unfamiliar with the term matrix. There are many structures in mathematics that can be treated singularly in binary operations despite their apparent complexity. Depending on how the structure enters into the operation its function has names such as divisor, multiplicand, transpose, etc. What all structures have in common is that they represent a state. It is the practice of mathematics to combine structures according to the laws of their operators such as addition and multiplication. Over the many years the mathematical structures evolved, adding more complexity as needed. A matrix structure can be considered to be yet another mathematical structure for which rules for binary operations are defined. Moreover, the structure is introduced here as an algebra. Any algebra has universally defined properties for the numerical structure and mathematical operations on it. To understand the requirements of an algebra refer to the article at wikiversity. The matrix structure and its mathematical operations allow these structures be an algebra.
Aside:  The grounds for Cayley's work actually began back in 1720 when Newton had recorded a method to solve simultaneous equations using a particular form into his personal notes that was later published with his disapproval as "Arithmetica Universalis". On page 60 is the section "Of the Transformation of two or more EQUATIONS into one, in order to exterminate the unknown Quantities." then followed on page 63 by the section "The Extermination of an unknown Quantity of several Dimensions in each Equation".
Aside:  A second prior work that contributed to the definition of matrices was done in 1810 when a procedural notation for the elimination process in solving simultaneous equations was defined by Carl Friedrich Gauss in his "Theory of the Motion of the Heavenly Bodies Moving about the Sun in Conic Sections". This can be found in the second book's third section "DETERMINATION OF AN ORBIT FROM ANY NUMBER OF OBSERVATIONS" part 184, starting on page 267.

[1861] James Clerk Maxwell  The unification of optics and electromagnetism.
In the late 1800's it was desired to understand the connection between magnetism and electricity. It was known from Michael Faraday's work that electric energy could produce magnetic energy. Around 1861 James Clerk Maxwell continued research on this relationship and produced equations that represented light as a form of an electromagnetic wave[10]. Maxwell's work was significant to subsequent investigations using spectral analysis in order to correlate the energy of emitted light to atomic structures.

[1872] Felix Klein   Introduction of Invariant Transformation Groups.
Nearly forty years after Hamilton's introduction of the new mechanics representation, Felix Klein published a series of mathemathical analyses that included the geometries of curved sufaces. Published in 1872, this collection of his analyses is called the "Erlangen programme". A part of this work was to depict a curved surface as the result of a group of linear transformations of successive coordinates along the surface. The transformation group produces successive coordinates that define the system's evolution. Klein's work produced a means to discover those elements of the mathematical model for the geometry that do not change under a group of transformations[29]. This work is collectively referred to as transformation geometry[39]. By removing the noise, what remains as invariant by the transformation group exposes the symmetry of the motion and the main forces that are conserved throughout an orbital path.

[1880] Albert A. Michelson  The Invention of the Interferometer.
During the decade of 1880 there were a number of significant events. One in particular was the invention of the interferometer. Spectroscopic instruments and their careful use have made important contributions within the historical investigations of the subatomic particles. Albert Michelson was a pioneer and major contributor to the field of spectroscopy. In 1881 Michelson invented the interferometer[49] and used it to make contribution to the field of Astrophysics by examining the spectral lines of the light originating from space. Michelson was searching for evidence of the aether wind of space by measuring the effect it had on the propagation of light. None was found. It is now known that space consists of a near vacuum with a small amount of hydrogen. This failed experiment was of special interest at that time because it was understood that other types of waves such as sound require some sort of medium (water or air) through which to propagate.

[1884] Josiah Gibbs  The definition of Dot and Inner Products from Quaternians.
Hamilton's quaternions algebra was later extended to vector algebra by Josiah Gibbs around 1884. Josiah identified the vector multiplication operators dot product and inner product[38]. The dot product of two vectors with real valued terms A=(a1,a2) and B=(b1,b2) is expressed as A·B=a1,b1+a2,b2[40]. The result of the dot product of two real valued vectors is a real value. The inner product of two vectors with complex valued terms A=(a1,a2) and B=(b1,b2), where each ai and bi has the form x+iy, is expressed as <A,B>=a1b1 + a2b2[41]. The overbar on the bi terms is notation common to Physics for the complex conjugate transpose. There is some confusion in field of mathematics wherein different branches of study use different notations for the same thing. Refer to the 'Notation' section of the Complex Conjugate article for more information on the differences. The resulting value of the inner product of two complex valued vectors is a complex value.

Binary operators like addition and multiplication have properties regarding the mathematical implications of the operator's terms and the impact that scalars have in association with the terms. Real valued terms for the inner product may be considered correct because the inner product is an extension of the dot product and is inclusive of the case of real valued terms. [1885] Johan Jakob Balmer  Formula For Visible Lines Of Hydrogen Spectrum.
The interferometer was used in 1885 by Johan Balmer to study the wavelength and spectral width of atomic hydrogen emission lines. Balmer found that the wavelengths of visible light from hydrogen, in particular the Hα, Hβ and Hγ lines, could be defined as a mathematical series λ=B*(n2 / [n2-22]), where B is the Balmer constant[50].

Aside:  When this measurement was done it was not known that hydrogen has just one electron. The observed spectral lines were not associated with an atomic structure. Electron particles were not identified until 1897 when J.J.Thomson's research with charged beams identified the electron particle. Much later, in 1913, Niels Bohr advanced a model for the atomic structure defining the atomic shells. According to the Bohr model, the hydrogen atom has numerous electron orbital shells designated by 'n'. The Bohr model allows the electron to be present in any one of the 'n' shells depending on its energy state. It is understood now that when the electron absorbs energy it is promoted from its current shell to the next. Also, when the electron emits a photon it looses energy that results in it being demoted to the next lower shell. In the case where a photon is emitted, the energy of the emitted photon will match the energy lost in the transition from the higher orbital to the lower one. Max Planck's work in 1900 related light frequency is to energy by E=hf. Therefore the spectral line's frequency 'f' will correspond to the particular energy loss by the orbital change.

[1887] Heinrich Hertz  Observation of the Photoelectric Effect.
In 1887 Heinrich Hertz was performing experiments with a spark-gap transmitter[15] and detector. His intent was to confirm the wave theory of electromagnitism proposed earlier by James Clerk Maxwell[11]. While performing his experiments he discovered that by shining light on the detector rods he could more readily reproduce the sparks from the generator. His data demonstrated the photoelectric effect. At that time he was not able to explain the effect that he discovered. His discovery would later influence Philipp Lenard's research and his model of atomic structure.

It is also interesting to note that Hertz characterized the spark gap transmitter used in his experiments to investigate the electromagnetic waves. His characterization of this device's resulting electromagnetic wave became well known. This influenced later work by Max Planck who proposed that black body radiation could be modeled mathematically by using a collection of hertzian oscillators[16].

[1888-1889] Albert Michelson  Fine Line Structure of Hydrogen Hα Spectral Line.
Albert Michelson was asked by the International Committee of Weights and Measures to find a way to measure the standard meter length using light waves. During the late 1800's he used the interferometer he invented to investigate what light source would be best to establish the number of wavelengths in the stanadard meter. During his research he examined the light from a hydrogen source. While studying the light from hydrogen he discovered that the Hα spectral line[52] displayed as two spectral lines, a doublet, rather than the expected single red line. This was the discovery of the fine structure of the hydrogen spectral lines[51].

Aside:  The importance of this discovery came into focus when Bohr proposed his atomic model in 1913. Atomic models are comprised of both a physical concept and an attendant mathematical description. It is an expectation that the model's fitness depends on its mathematical prediction of observable effects. The Bohr atomic model of 1913 defined quantized energy levels as atomic shells he designated with 'n'. Bohr modeled electron orbitals as symmetrically circular paths. Because the energy and angular momentum in the Bohr model is constant it is then expected that the frequency 'f' of the spectral lines would always be single lines. So the presence of a doublet becomes significant as Physicists begin to work on the fitness of subsequent atomic models. This specific issue with the atomic model was addressed by Arnold Sommerfeld in 1915 who identified the azimuthal angle.

[1892] Hendrik A. Lorentz  Theory of the Electron.
Up to the 1800s there was a significant refinement in the understanding of electric and magnetic forces from the works of the famous physicists such as Gilbert in in 1600, Franklin in 1747, Volta in 1769, Oersted in 1820, Faraday in 1821, Ampère in 1827, Ohm in 1827, Maxwell in 1873, Heaviside in 1884, and others. By 1892, during Lorentz's investigations of the ether (Michelson's 1880 'aether wind of space'), he pulled together the properties associated with electricity, magnetism and a theory of electrons. In his time the electron was not regarded as physical particle. It isn't until five years later in 1897 that the electron mass was measured by J.J.Thomson. Lorentz developed the relationship between electrical charge, heat, and light in his theory of the electrons. He published his works as a series of lectures he delivered at Columbia University in New York[58].

[1893] Wilhelm Wien  Correlation Between Energy and Frequency of Emitted Light.
A number of investigations were being performed on light emissions from black bodies[5] during the late 1800's. Wilhelm Wien's displacement law of 1893 established that the peak energy of the wavelengths of light emitted from a black body "shifted" as a function of the cavity's temperature[6] (applied energy). Moreover, Wien reported that the product of the wavelength having the peak emission energy and its temperature was the constant value (0.2898 centimetre-degree Kelvin). An explanation for Wien's law was later proposed in 1900 by Max Planck.

[1896] Peter Zeeman  Discovery of Magnetic Impact On Light.
Zeeman studied the effect of light from burning sodium metal emitted through a narrow slit and reflected by a concave grating[47]. His experiments demonstrated that the sodium light was split mainly into two separate sharp yellow lines. He characterized the lines as resulting mainly from two dominant oscillations of the sodium metal's atomic structure. He then applied a large magnetic field across the burning sodium metal. The slit was aligned perpendicular to the applied magnetic field's lines of force. The previously focused beams became diffused. Zeeman understood from Hendrik A. Lorentz's teaching that the sodium contained electrons. According to Lorentz's theory, the light oscillations that create the focused beams (without the external magnetic field) resulted from the normal vibration of the electrons[48]. Zeeman summarized from his experimental evidence that the magnetic field created new oscillations in the sodium having greater and lesser periods of oscillation. The diffusion of the beams, he concluded, was a result of the magnetic field displacing the electrons from their normal vibrations.

The discovery by Zeeman became known as the Zeeman effect. It had a subsequent contribution to the atomic model through the work of Otto Stern in 1921.

[1897] Joseph J. Thomson  First Measurement of Electrons and First Model of Atomic Structure.
By the early 1800s the concepts of charge, current and voltage were understood.[2] But the particles involved in these properties were not. The electron is the first of the subatomic particles identified[3]. Joseph J. Thomson's work with beams in a cathode ray tube in 1897 discovered that a ratio existed between an applied electric charge and the mass of the beam. Thomson proposed that the beam in his experiment was composed of individual particles having a mass approximately 1/1,700 of that of the hydrogen atom. The significance of this work established that a particle existed that was smaller than an atom (sub-atomic) having its own measurable mass. He also reasoned that since the electron has a negative charge and the gas element has no charge then something else existed that neutralized the charge of the electron. He called this a neutron and envisioned it to be the balance of the atom's structure.

Aside:  Up to this point the notions of physics regarding planetary orbitals, light spectrums, magnetism, charge and more have been following along the understood principles of classical physical behaviors. The mathematical analysis used has been based on the assumption of a continuous analog behavior. All of that is suddenly changed by Max Planck who in 1900 introduced the concept that is the entire point of this historical development. That point being that the physical behavior at the subatomic level demonstrates a discrete quantized property that is called QUANTUM.

[1900] Max Planck  First Expression of Quantized Energy.
Max Planck worked with the physics of thermodynamics and black body radiation. He sought to explain Wilhelm Wien's black body radiation experiments that demonstrated a shift in wavelength proportional to the applied heat. Influenced by previous work by Heinrich Hertz on the photoelectric effect, he proposed that the observed shift could be explained in terms of atomic vibrations that he modeled using harmonic oscillators[16]. While developing his mathematical model Max Planck discovered that it was necessary to establish that the energy change progressed in "discrete" units that he called the energy element 'E'. The discrete energy units were the minimal increment of energy 'E' for each Hertzian oscillator in his model. To adjust his mathematics to match Wien's earlier account of the relationship between the energy and frequency he deduced a porportionality constant 'h' [7]. This constant is called Planck's constant. Max Planck expressed the relationship between energy and frequency as E=hf. The concept of a discrete energy element 'E' was in conflict with the continuous analog gain or loss of energy understood within physics at that time. Planck's work introduced the important concept of "quantization of energy" but it remained for Einstein to later rationalize this concept. Einstein redefined the concept of the discrete energy element 'E' to be the minimal energy of an electromagnetic wave, or photon.

In his analysis of the spectral density of black body radiation his model was based on a volume of Hertzian oscillators, each of which uniquely contributed to the total energy. This approach was later regarded by Satyendra Bose to lack proper conformity to the quantum principal of discrete quantities. Bose used a statistical model to generate the same black body spectral densities.

Aside:  In the equation E=hf the energy is related to frequency using Planck's constant h. To relate energy of an orbital to its angular frequency the relationship ω=2πf can be used to replace linear frequency using ω/2π=f. That gives E=hω/2π. The normalized Planck's constant ℏ is h/2π so for convenience this is typically expressed as E=ℏω.

[1902] Philipp Lenard  Photoelectric Correlation Between Applied Light Frequency and Electron Energy.
Further study of the photoelectric effect took place in 1902 by Philipp Lenard. Lenard's work with the photoelectric effect introduced another confirmation that atoms consisted of subatomic particles [electrons] and asserted that the atom structure consisted mostly of empty space. Philipp Lenard also presented his atom model as neutrally charged having positively charged components that balanced the negative charge of the electrons. His experimental data with electron emissions by incident light demonstrated that the amount of energy of the emitted electrons was not a function of the light intensity, as he expected, but rather the energy was greater for the higher frequencies of applied light[12]. This finding conformed well with the earlier result of Max Planck's research that stated E=hf. The higher frequency "f" of the light imparts greater energy "E" to the surface molecules of the illuminated material.

[1905] Albert Einstein  Unification of Wave and Planck's Quanta Models.
In 1905 Albert Einstein had an extraordinary year[14] in which he published a number of significant articles. The prior work done by James Clerk Maxwell, Heinrich Hertz and Philipp Lenard was strongly based on the wave property of light. Max Planck's work in 1900 introduced the idea that energy was somehow quantized. The wave and quanta ideas did not seem to align. Einstein understood the physical implications of both Max Planck's quanta and Maxwell's wave theory. In this productive year Einstein determined that the wave and particle models could coexist if the wave energy was considered to be an average over many quantized packets. Einstein then applied this concept to more accurately explain the physics of the photoelectric effect. He used the electron subatomic particle and atom model introduced by J. J. Thomson in 1897. Einstein proposed that Planck's quanta of energy was a particle of light. Einstein envisioned that particles of light (photons) colliding with the atoms's electrons in the metallic element caused the electrons to be released from their atomic orbital to become radiated electrons[13]. In this work Einstein introduced the wave-particle duality and the law of the photoelectric effect. He received the Nobel prize in 1921 for the law of the photoelectric effect.

[1905] Albert Einstein  Theory of Relativity.
In June of 1905 Einstein published the paper "ON THE ELECTRODYNAMICS OF MOVING BODIES"[53]. This contained his theory of relativity in which he used a moving rod metaphor observed in two distinct frames of reference. He first established a definition of simultaneity by describing the synchronization of two clocks A and B that are used to establish the time of two linked events, each event at their different locations. Each clock transmits the time of its event to the other clock position using a beam of light. Some data is acquired by an observer at each clock at the synchronized time. It is significant to be able to compare the results of the following frames that the time for the light to travel in both directions is the same. Einstein then introduced his moving rod metaphor. The moving rod can be regarded as rather long or else the observers rather small. At each end of the rod is a pair of synchronized clocks awaiting their event to transmit the time. Each clock has an observer moving with it. Someplace forward of the moving rod are two more synchronized clocks that are synchronized with the two on the rod. Each fixed clock has a stationary observer. At a simultaneous time t an event triggers the four clocks so the four observers can record the precise position of the rod ends with respect to a parallel coordinate system. The observers in motion have some velocity v in their frame of reference that impacts the time t that it takes for the light to travel in either direction. Einstein asserts this to be true because regardless of the rod's velocity and relative motion of its clocks, the light wave propagates at an independent constant velocity c. Because clock B is moving away from the light at the velocity v the net velocity in the motion frame is reduced to c-v, increasing the duration of time of light travel between the fixed points A and B. For the same reason the velocity in the direction B to A is increased to c+v, decreasing the duration of time of light traveling back. But in the stationary frame there is zero velocity and so the light travel times are equal in either direction. The conclusion is that the observers in motion frame would declare that the clocks were not synchronized by the definition of synchronization given and more importantly, that measurements are relative to their frame of reference.

[1905] Albert Einstein  Relativistic Mass.
In September of 1905 Einstein published the short paper "DOES THE INERTIA OF A BODY DEPEND UPON ITS ENERGY-CONTENT?"[54] It was in this publication that Einstein's famous equation E=mc2 is credited. The actual point of his publication was that kinetic energy of a moving body viewed from two frames of reference will be different. His reasoning follows directly from his previous paper on his theory of relativity. He begins with an arbitrary body positioned in a three dimensional coordinate system (x,y,z) as a rest frame of reference. A moving frame having coordinates (ξ,η,ζ) that is parallel to the rest frame is a second frame of reference. Within the rest frame the body's energy is depicted as E0 and in the motion frame the energy is depicted as H0. If the observed body emits a portion of its energy as light then the amount of energy remaining is different depending on the frame. The total energy of the body is its kinetic plus its potential. To preserve the potential energy Einstein proposed that the emission energy within the rest frame is composed of two equal and opposite emissions ½L. In the rest frame the remaining energy is E1 and the motion frame it is H1. The difference between the two energy values observed in the two frames H-E is then related to differences of their kinetic energy K. In each frame the original energy is equal to the sum of remaining energy plus the kinetic energy lost by the emission. Because the emitted energy was defined for the rest frame, the emission energy ½L must be translated to the motion coordinates by the angle between the two frames as the cos(θ). He performs several steps of algebraic manipulation to represent the difference between the kinetic energy of the two frames K0-K1. His final representation of the kinetic energy difference between the two frames is given as K0-K1=½(L/[c2])v2. Kinetic energy is related to mass and velocity of the body as K=½mV2. In Einstein's final representation it can be seen that (L/[c2]) can be viewed as m. Set m=(L/[c2]) and rearranging reveals L=m[c2]. Recall that L was the total energy loss of the body, so E=mc2. Einstein's conclusion (from the English translation found on John Walker's Formilab web site[54]) was that, "The mass of a body is a measure of its energy-content; if the energy changes by L, the mass changes in the same sense by L/(9 × 1020)...". The contribution of this work is the establishment in special relativity of a rest mass and a relativistic mass[59].

[1911] Ernest Rutherford  Planetary Atomic Model.
Subsequent research was being done to better formalize the atomic model produced by J. J. Thomson's work. The nucleus was formally demonstrated in 1911[4] and credited to Ernest Rutherford. He experimented with directing alpha particles at a thin foil of gold metal. His experiment was to investigate scattering of the alpha particles by the atomic structure of the foil. The experimental data collected was comprised largely of no deflections at all, had a few deflections and most surprisingly had some very rare reflections. The conclusion by the near absence of deflections and the presence of rare reflections was that a small region at the center of the atomic structure called the nucleus contained the majority of its mass and positive charge and the rest of the atom was empty. This new model of the atom is called the planetary model. This was the next step towards establishing a physical model for the atom's subatomic particles.

Aside:  Rutherford created alpha particles from radon gas, a radioactive element for which he is credited with the discovery[64]. As the radon decays it emits alpha particles. Rutherford found three types of radioactive particles; alpha, beta and gamma. The three types were classified by the degree of penetration into thin foils of metals[65].
Aside:  After the introduction of a 'quantization of energy' in 1900 by Max Planck, the next significant steps for Quantum Mechanics was the identification of specific quantized properties and their Quantum Numbers. In 1913 Niels Bohr began the process of defining these quantum numbers starting with 'n'. The four quantum properties of the electron are represented by the letters n "shell number", l "azimuthal number", m "magnetic number" and s "spin number".

[1913] Niels Bohr  Bohr Atomic Model with Atomic Shells and First Quantum Number.
Rutherford's planetary model gained acceptance. However the manner of the relationship between the nucleus and the electrons needed to be investigated. Niels Bohr knew that the electron particle moving on a circular trajectory around a nucleus would loose energy and its orbit would decay over time. Bohr proposed a new version of the planetary model in 1913[8]. Bohr's work followed on Einstein's clarification of Max Planck's pioneer work with quantization of energy. The Bohr atomic model positioned the electrons into discrete circular orbitals associated with fixed energy levels. He referred to the circular orbitals as shells and assigned them numbers. The orbital number 'n' was the first identified quantum number assigned to the electron. The smaller orbitals of the Bohr model were assigned the lesser energy levels and the largest orbitals the greater energy levels. An electron with a particular energy level resulted in the electron occupying a particular orbital. As an electron gained or lost sufficient energy it could shift to a higher or lower orbit. Bohr also reasoned out that each shell can contain a number of electrons with the same energy level.

To explain the location of the shells, Bohr proposed that the electron's energy (angular velocity) and the axial force of charge attraction between the electron and the nucleus holding it in the particular shell determined the radius of a circular arc. J.J.Thomson previously published the electron's mass. Max Planck previously published his Energy Quanta. Bohr computed a size for the atomic orbitals as a function of the energy element 'E', the electron mass and Planck's constant h.[9] In summary, the Bohr model of orbitals was a series of discrete energy states where the energy difference of the transition from one state to another is either emitted or absorbed as a light quantum.

Aside:  In 1686 Isaac Newton established his laws of motion. The first law is famously, "Every object persists in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless it is compelled to change that state by forces impressed on it." Momentum is the conserved linear property of an object (mass) moving in a straight line and at a particular speed (velocity) that preserves the object's motion in the straight line. The linear momentum is equal to the mass times the velocity (p=mv) and the value p is a linear momentum vector having computed magnitude in the same direction as the velocity vector. For circular motion the momentum is expressed as the angular momentum and is the conserved angular property of the mass moving in the circular path.
     The angular momentum is the cross product of its position vector r and its perpendicular linear momentum vector (r x mv). Because angular momentum is a cross product of two vectors, the resulting vector is perpendicular to both the position vector r and velocity vector v. The resulting direction of the cross product is given by the right-hand rule. Linear momentum has a moment that is defined as the 1st moment of momentum. In general a moment is defined by μn=rnQ. The 1st moment of momentum is that value for which n=1 and Q is the applied force. The moment is then a vector having the same direction as the applied force. The torque is similar but is defined as τ=r x F, where r is a position vector rather than a length. Given the difference between moment and torque, torque can be considered the angular momentum's equivalent to linear momentum's moment.

[1915] Arnold Sommerfeld  Azimuth Axis and Second Quantum Number.
Both Bohr and Sommerfeld were aware of the conflict of Bohr's orbital model with the fine line doublet of the Hα spectral line discovered by Albert Michelson in 1888. If the electron orbit was circular as proposed by Bohr then the angular velocity would be constant and the expected spectral display of the light emissions would always be single lines. In 1915 Arnold Sommerfeld proposed a new model to address the existence of Michelson's fine line structure. He apparently reasoned out that in order to have photon emission with discrete wavelengths slightly offset in frequency from the main Hα emission line while having the same shell 'n', the model required that the electron's orbital energy must be slightly shifted in some instances. For a more informative explanation refer to PHYSICS HIGHER SECONDARY SECOND YEAR VOLUME - II, TAMIL NADU TEXTBOOK CORPORATION, COLLEGE ROAD, CHENNAI - 600 00: starting on page 23, Section 6.2.10 'Sommerfeld atom model'.

Sommerfeld proposed that the electrons travel about the positively charged nucleus in elliptical orbits rather than the circular orbits of the Bohr model. He established two foci for the ellipse, one for the apogee and one for the perigee, and placed the nucleus at the apogee. His model envisioned that the semi-major axis would increase as the orbital 'n' increased. At the lowest energy orbital the Bohr's circular orbit was then a special case.

Aside:  Sommerfeld's introduction of elliptical[62] orbits to the atomic structure had a parallel in prior history. Before 1609 the standing theory of planetary orbits was the Copernican model that stated that all planetary motion was in circular orbits around the sun. In 1609 Johannes Kepler published his law of planetary orbits[56] that disagreed with the Copernican model. Kepler's new law of planetary orbits established that they actually travel in elliptical paths[57].

Most important to his new model, Sommerfeld understood Einstein's 1905 publication of relativistic mass. Because the electron orbital speed (oscillation by earlier accounts) is at near light speed the relativistic mass has an observable effect. His model allowed for the electron's velocity to vary throughout the elliptical orbit where the electron's velocity was at its greatest in its closest distance to the nucleus and slowest at its greatest distance from the nucleus. His model's variation in velocity introduced a variation in the electron's angular momentum throughout its orbit. Accordingly, the relativistic mass of the electron should vary as its speed varies throughout the elliptical orbit. The introduction of mass variance added slight deflections out of the flat plane of the orbit[19]. Sommerfeld defined these precessed orbitals as having discrete offset angles that produced slight shifts in energy corresponding to the discrete fine lines observed by Michelson. Each angular offset constitutes an azimuthal angle out of the flat plane of orbit and each has slightly different effective energy. The introduction of the discrete azimuthal angle is the second identified quantum number for electrons and is typically represented as 'l'. Because Sommerfeld's model exploited relativistic mass it is sometimes referred to as the relativistic atomic model.

Aside:  Azimuthal may be an unfamiliar term. Azimuthal refers to an angular displacement from a line of sight or straight line. Typical use of this relates to marine navigation where a sextant is used to determine longitude and latitude. The instrument was introduced around 1731[55]. The following is a very abbreviated description. A much better explanation can be found at http://www.marineinsight.com/marine-navigation/what-is-a-sextant/. The sextant instrument can display two images simultaneously side by side. The first sighting is with the telescope to view the horizon and establish the flat plane of travel. The sextant's index bar is then adjusted to view a celestial reference object through the index mirror such that its image aligns with the horizon. The angle between the two objects is the azimuthal angle and it is correlated with the time of the observation to determine position from reference charts.

[1921] Otto Stern  Magnetic Moment and Third Quantum Number.
Otto Stern noted that electrons of the hydrogen atom moving in a rotational orbit around the nucleus should produce a magnetic moment in the same way that current passed through a circular coil of wire does. That was not supported by the evidence. To investigate this anomaly the Stern–Gerlach experiment was performed. Charged particle beams had been used previously by Joseph J. Thomson in 1897 in his research that exposed the ratio between an applied electric charge and the mass of the beam. Also prior to the Stern-Gerlach experiment, in 1911 Louis Dunoyer experimented with the luminescence properties of sodium vapors. Dunoyer discovered that by using the optical slit experimental methods, he was able to create a straight line beam of neutral sodium atoms[44]. Dunoyer's experiment provided proof of the kinetic theory of gases[45]. Otto Stern was aware of Dunoyer's neutral atomic beam apparatus[46].

In the Stern-Gerlach experiment a beam of silver atoms was passed through a nonuniform magnetic field. The expectation was that the silver atoms would behave as tiny magnets, interact with the magnetic field, and the beam would become diffused. Rather than a diffuse deposition on the collector of the atomic beam apparatus, the beam split into only discrete points. This demonstrated that the magnetic moment of an atom in an external magnetic field can have only a few possible discrete orientation angles with respect to the external field lines. The magnetic quantum number of an electron is a discrete integer value that is a function of the electron's angular momentum quantum number l. The magnetic quantum number ml became the third quantum number assigned to the electron.

[1924] Satyendra Nath Bose  Formulation of Quantum Statistics
The theoretical understanding of quantum states in 1924 was that each state was a strict consequence of the quantum numbers such that the particles strictly adhered to one of the available quantum states. In this model the quantum particles were presumed to be uniquely distinguishable. Max Planck had previously used energy quantum and unique quantum states in his mathematical model[66] to explain Wilhelm Wien's black body radiation. His radiation formula defined the amount of emitted radiation (spectral density) of a surface maintained at a constant temperature. He used a set of unique harmonic oscillators, each one being distinguishable from each other.

It was significant to Bose that the unique harmonic oscillators entered into Planck's mathematical model as distinguishable contributors[67].  Additionally, Bose objected to the use of classical physics in order to obtain the spectral density formula for black body radiation. He was able to derive the same result as Planck by using a statistical derivation of the density of a black-body spectral emission. In his publication "PLANCK'S LAW AND THE LIGHT QUANTUM HYPOTHESIS" in the American Journal of Physics he explained his approach. An important aspect of his work was later called the B-E statistics that was based on his introduction of indistinguishable particles. Because of the significance of the introduction of statistical physics I present a more rigorous examination of Bose's publication in the article ~Statistics.

[1924] Louis de Broglie  The de Broglie Wavelength.
In 1924 Louis de Broglie theorized that all matter exhibits both particle and wave properties[20]. He extended the particle wave duality of the photon proposed by Einstein to all matter. This property of matter is called matter waves[21]. He specified that the electron particle can be described by an intrinsic "phase wave" which propogates the electron in a specific orbital. Louis de Broglie correlated the wave and orbital number by stating that a whole number of the electron's wavelengths must fit onto the circumference of its orbital shell.

[1925] Werner Heisenberg  Electron Orbitals as Statistical Distributions.
Heisenberg remained skeptical about the theories regarding an atomic orbital. He rejected the notion by the Bohr model of a neatly fixed orbital at a fixed radius. Heisenberg proposed that if the electron behaved like a wave then it is not possible to know its position and momentum precisely at the same instance in time[32]. His desire was to express the mechanics of electron orbitals solely on measurable properties. He reasoned that the electron's mechanics could be explained by making a correlation between the observable emission and absorption of light using spectroscopy[31]. At the time the orbital distance was regarded as a function of its momentum as related to Bohr's energy element E, Thomson's mass and Planck's constant h. Heisenberg's approach to electron orbitals was more a statistical representation by using data from spectroscopy measurements over time and expressed the transitions by square arrays. The matrix algebra employed had been defined by Cayley and Sylvester in 1858. Instead of describing the motion as a coordinate system with position being some function of time, x(t), he used a square array of transition amplitudes to represent the orbit[34]. This is referred to in some literature as Heisenberg's matrix mechanics[42].

[1926] Max Born  Statistical Transition Between Electron Orbitals.
Max Born realized that, just as orbitals have a statistical field associated with them, the promotion or demotion of electrons between the fields is not a precise energy value. Rather the change of electron orbitals has a statistical probability having some range of energy values. In June of 1926 Max Born submitted an article to Zeitschrift für Physik that was published later that year in Volume 37, Issue 12, pp.863-867 titled 'Zur Quantenmechanik der Stoßvorgänge', in English "On the quantum mechanics of collisions". In this paper he proposed a statistical approach to the understanding of atomic orbitals and the likely promotion or demotion of an electron from one orbital to another called the Born Rule[75]. Born expressed his position on classical physics in the following extract from his lecture presented to the Nobel Prize committee in 1954, "... the determinism of classical physics turns out to be an illusion, created by overrating mathematico-logical concepts. It is an idol, not an ideal in scientific research and cannot, therefore, be used as an objection to the essentially indeterministic statistical interpretation of quantum mechanics."[35]

[1926] Erwin Rudolf Josef Alexander Schrödinger  Introduction of Orbital Wave Mechanics.
Erwin Schrödinger accepted that the matter wave duality was real. In 1926 he published a wave equation based on the wave property to describe the statistical distribution of electron orbitals based on the wave property of electron particles. His equation is often referred to as the Schrödinger wave equation. In his equation he set about to transform the prevailing orbital model of a uniform curved path into a system of wave surfaces normal to it.

  Neither the dot product nor the inner product operator is associative because the dot and inner product operations between a vector and a scalar is not defined. Each operator is only valid between two vectors. However there is a kind of associative definition involving a scalar as shown. The equality is a combination of the multiply and dot product or inner product operators. A vector's magnitude is scaled by multiplication with a scaler. For this associative equality to hold either A or B can be scaled but not both. The right side of the equality shown scales the B vector by a, but it also holds if the A vector is scaled.
  The inner product has its origin in Hamilton's quest for multiplication of complex valued vectors. The imaginary component of the complex valued vector presents a special case for this operation. In the case of the dot product from which the inner product is derived, two real valued vectors are the terms of the operation and the result is a single number. That number represents what magnitude of the first real valued vector exists in the same direction as the second vector. Ideally the Inner Product binary operator should be commutative the same as the Dot Product, however the commutative property only holds for the complex conjugate of the commuted operation.
